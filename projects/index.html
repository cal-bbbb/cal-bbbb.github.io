Calum Boone, otherwise known as Cal B., is a singer/songwriter, producer, and artist from Toronto, Canada. At a young age, he moved to the US, living in various places along the east coast where he would be exposed to various types of music. Despite coming from a family with no musical background, Cal B. was involved in music from a young age, taking piano, violin, trumpet, and vocal lessons (albeit for a very insignificant period of time) to help him get involved.

By high school, Cal B. was experimenting with music production in softwares like FL Studio and GarageBand, creating weird little beats that sounded good at the time (obviously, they were awful). By the time he was in university, he was hooked, electing to spend most of his nights creating beats instead of sleeping. By 2020, he had released his first song, a feature on a Yung Rama track. After countless hours watching YouTube and learning the ways of audio engineering, Cal B. dropped his debut single, Slow Down. Once the pandemic wrapped up, Cal B. performed a few backyard and basement shows. It was there that he would discover his love for having a microphone in hand.

Now, Cal B. spends his time honing his engineering skills and, when he gets around to it, recording and releasing the occasional record with his friends.

Hi

"I am an artist. I am an artist. I am an artist. Still doesn't feel real. While I do tend to consider myself part of many musical communities, I think one of the biggest that I'm part of is from my current budding music career, through which I've had the opportunity to make songs with many different artists. Through my fraternity, we hosted concerts at people's houses, collaborated on songs, and built a local following of devoted fans to sing along whenever we play our music. It's been something incredibly special to take part in all this, and it definitely drives a feeling of community, but also a sense of being part of something larger than you-- something I don't often experience. I can still remember how nervous I was for the first show, especially how much I drank to overcome that, but getting on that "stage" (it was just the floor) was absolutely exhilarating. Going on a rollercoaster is thrilling, sure, but it felt like nothing compared to this. The difference is that when you're on a rollercoaster, you have a reasonable expectation that you will end up where you started after going around the track. It's safe. Performing, however, is not. There's no safety net, no braking system to take over if you embarrass yourself. And that's precisely what makes it so powerful. Just 30 humans in a room with an amplifier, building magic out of nothing."
